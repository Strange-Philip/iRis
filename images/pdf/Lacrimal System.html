THE TEAR FILM

OVERVIEW

The tear film overlays the ocular surface, which is comprised of the corneal and conjunctival epithelia, and provides the interface between these epithelia and the external environment. The tear film is essential for the health and protection of the ocular surface and for clear vision as the tear film is the first refractive surface of the eye.

The tear film has a thickness of 7 to 10µm, with the aqueous layer accounting for 90% of the thickness. volume of 7.4 µl in
unanaesthesized eye, 2.6 µl in anaesthesized eye and decreases with age.
It is grossly visible at the lower lid margin and is optically clear and is continuosly renewed.It is composed of proteins & mucoproteins to maintain its wetting and viscosity necessary for health of the epithelium of the ocular surface.

FUNCTIONS OF THE TEAR FILM

1. Keeps the surface moist and serves as a lubricant between the globe and eyelids

2. Traps debris and helps remove sloughed epithelial cells and debris

3. Is the primary source of atmospheric oxygen for the cornea

4. Provides a smooth refractive surface necessary for optimum optical function

5. contains antibacterial substances (lysozyme, beta-lysin,lactoferrin, immunoglobulins) to help protect against infection

6. helps to maintain corneal hydration by changes in tonicity that occur with evaporation

7. contains various growth factors and peptides that can regulate ocular surface wound repair.

1. The precorneal tear film is 3.4micrometers thick ,7.4 microliters in volume and 2.6 microliters when anaesthesized.It was known to be composed of three layers;

a. The mucin layer;

  • Deepest layer,0.02-0.05µm thick

· Helps to anchor the tear film to the ocular surface

  • Prevents adherence of infective organism

· Lubricates ocular and palpebral surfaces and reduces friction

· It offers a slippery coating over foreign bodies and protecting cornea and conjunctiva against abrasive effects

b. The aqueous layer;

· This layer forms the main bulk of the tear,with a thickness of 10µm.

· Contains inorganic salts, electrolytes (Na+ K+ Mg2+, Ca2+, Cl,HCO -3, PO43-), Water,glucose, urea, enzymes, proteins(Over 60 proteins), glycoproteins, and most of the antibacterial substances.

· It is secreted by the main and accessory lacrimal glands(Wolfring and Krausse).

  • It has a pH of 6.5 – 7.6

· The protein protect the ocular surface from bacterial infection

TEARS

It is clear watery fluid secreted by the lacrimal gland which along with the secretions from the meibomian glands,the gland of Zeis and Moll,the goblet cells and the accessory lacrimal glands of Krause and Wolfring helps to maintain the cornea and the conjunctiva moist and healthy. The normal tear volume is about 7 ±2 µL in each eye.

COMPOSITION OF TEAR

• The average pH of tears is 7.35, although a wide normal variation exists (5.20–8.35).

• Under normal conditions, tear fluid is isotonic. Tear film osmolarity ranges from 295 to 309 mosm/L

• Osmolarity-Normal osmotic pressure is equivalent to 0.9% NaCl solution

• Water as the major component of 98.2%

• Electrolytes-Na+,Ca2+,Cl-,Mg 2+,K+,HCO3-,Mn+;with sodium and chloride present in high concentration

• Vitamins-Vitamin A and Vitamin C

• Albumin constitutes about 60 % of the total protein.

• Immunoglobulins IgA, IgG, and IgE as well as lysozymes make up the remaining 40 %.

a) IgA predominates and differs from serum IgA in that it not only transudates from serum but is produced by plasma cells located in the lacrimal gland.

b) In vernal conjunctivitis, the IgE concentration of tears increases.

c) Tear lysozymes form 21 to 25 % of the total proteins,a proteolytic enzyme,net positive charge,produced by acinar cells of lacrimal gland and acting synergistically with gamma globulins and other non-lysozyme antibacterial factors, they represent an important defense mechanism against infection(bacteria;destroys the cell wall of bacteria).

• Tears have a small amount of glucose (5 mg/dL) and urea (0.04 mg/dL), and changes in blood concentration produces changes in tear glucose
and urea levels.

• Cells-Include conjunctival,corneal keratinized epithelial cells,polymorphs,eosinophils and lymphocytes

• Lipids-Cholesterol esters,fatty acids and free cholesterols etc.

• Glycolytic enzymes,lactate dehydrogenase

• Betalysin-Antibacterial agent

• Mucopoly saccharides

• Mucin(Glycoproteins)

• Amino acids

• Metabolites-Glucose,lactate,pyruvate,urea.

SECRETIONS OF TEAR

The average rate of normal tear secretion is 30-120 microliters per hour or 0.5 -2.2 micro litres per minute. Premature infants fail to secrete tears, while newborn babies secrete tears in the 1st 24 hrs. In young adults, it is 2 micro litres per minute, as where as in elderly individual is I micro litre per minute.

TYPES OF TEAR SECRETIONS

• Basic tear secretion;

a. It occurs normally without any stimulations and no innervation.

b. Its source are accessory lacrimal glands of Krause and Wolfring, goblet cells & tarsal glands

c. It is responsible for maintenance of moistness of cornea and conjunctiva.

d. It is reduced in elderly people and in dry eye.

• Reflex tear secretion;

a. It occurs in response to an irritant, i.e.stimulation.

b. It also dependant on psychological(emotional)factors

c. It produced mainly by lacrimal gland and controlled by sympathetic & parasympathetic (7th Cranial Nerve) supply.

1) Sympathetic :

· act through vasomotor constriction or dilation

· non-medullated nerve fibers from the internal carotid plexus reach the gland either around the lacrimal artery or as nerve of pterygoid canal to sphenopalatine ganglion → zygomatic nerve → lacrimal gland.

2) Parasympathetic:

Acts from the superior salivary or lacrimatory nucleus in the brain stem →nervus intermedius (sensory root of facial) → greater superficial petrosal nerve → join nerve of pterygoid canal → relay in sphenopalatine ganglion → postganglionic fibers → zygomatic nerve →lacrimal gland.

* Afferent stimulus for reflex tearing:

i. optic nerve.

ii. trigeminal nerve.

iii. facial nerve (sensory part): taste and peppery food.

iv. synkinesis with vagus and glossopharyngeal nerves: tearing with yawing, coughing or vomiting.

v. Psychic: with emotions.

ELIMINATION OF TEARS

Tears are eliminated from the eye either by;

a. Evaporation

25% of secreted tears are eliminated when eyes are opened (no evaporation when eyes are closed). Evaporation leads to an increase in the tonicity of precorneal tear film as water is absorb from the cornea which may result in corneal dehydration.

b. Drainage through the nasolacrimal system

• 75% of secreted tears are eliminated through this system

ü Upper puncta (30-35%)

ü Lower puncta (65-70%)

• The tear drains through the lacrimal puncta into the canaliculi then into the lacrimal sac into the nasolacrimal duct then into the nose. This is done by some mechanism;


a) Passive: by gravity and capillary attraction.
b) Active (lid movement = suction pump):

· orbicularis contraction during lid closure: dilate the canaliculi and draw the lateral wall
of the sac laterally creating a negative pressure and tears are directed to the sac.

· orbicularis relaxation during lid opening: ampulla of vertical part of canaliculus dilates and the sac collapse and tears are
directed to the nasolacrimal duct.

· Folds or valves present in the nasolacrimal duct prevent
the air within the nose from being drawn up into the drainage system.

The active mechanism has been simplified in a short note detail to illustrate on tear drainage;

During closure, the eyelids meet first at the temporal canthus; closure then moves toward the medial canthus, where the tears pool in the lacrimal lake. The tear menisci are pushed toward the lacrimal puncta into which they drain.

Theories explaining tear drainage report that the state of the lacrimal sac is either distended or compressed by contraction of the orbicularis muscle. According to some, contraction of the lacrimal part of the orbicularis compresses the canaliculi, forcing the tears into the lacrimal sac.

Coincidentally, contraction of the muscle pulls on the fascial sheath attached to the lacrimal sac, which causes lateral displacement of the lateral wall, expanding the sac and creating negative pressure within it; in effect, pulling tears in from the canaliculus. On relaxation of the orbicularis, the lacrimal sac collapses, and the tears are driven into the nasolacrimal duct.

In addition, the canaliculi open and act as siphons to pull tears in through the puncta. The tears drain into the nasolacrimal duct mainly by gravity, where most are absorbed by the mucosal lining before the remaining tears enter the inferior meatus. A number of studies, however, have measured an increase in pressure within the lacrimal sac during lid closure.

Using high-speed photography, the medial edges of the eyelids have been observed to meet, halfway into a blink, occluding the puncta. According to this theory, the canaliculi and lacrimal sac are compressed, forcing all fluids into the nasolacrimal duct.

As the eyelids open, compression of the canaliculi decreases, but the puncta remain occluded, creating a negative pressure in the canaliculi. When the puncta finally are opened, the negative pressure pulls the tears in immediately after the blink.

The primary difference in these two theories is the state of the lacrimal sac. In the first scenario the sac is dilated, whereas in the second case the sac is compressed with orbicularis contraction. Capillary attraction plays a role in moving tears into the puncta and down into the canaliculi between blinks.

TEAR FILM BREAK UP

The normal time is 15 s. Measurement of the tear film breakup time may be useful to estimate the mucin content of tears. For the method, apply a slightly moistened
fluorescein strip to the bulbar conjunctiva and ask the patient to blink.

The tear film is then scanned with a cobalt filter on the slit lamp while the patient refrains from blinking. The time that elapses before the first dry spot that appears in the corneal fluorescein layer is the “tear film breakup time.” It is definitely reduced in eyes with mucin deficiency.

THE LACRIMAL APPARATUS

OVERVIEW

The structures responsible for the production and drainage of tears are collectively called the lacrimal apparatus. The lacrimal apparatus consists of two divisions: the secretory and the excretory part.

Fig 4 shows the two divisions of the lacrimal apparatus(secretory and excretory part)

THE LACRIMAL SECRETORY SYSTEM

The secretory system includes;

1. The main lacrimal gland:

a. Are paired, almond-shaped exocrine glands, one for each eye, that secretes the aqueous layer of the tear film.

b. They are situated in the upper lateral region of each orbit, in the lacrimal fossa of the orbit formed by the frontal bone.

2. The accessory lacrimal glands which consist of:

a. Glands of Zeis and Gland of moll;

Glands of Zeis are unilobar sebaceous glands located on the margin of the eyelid and it services the eyelash.

The Glands of Moll are apocrine glands located in the same area near the eyelid, near the base of the eyelashes.

These glands produce an oily substance that is issued through the excretory ducts of the sebaceous lobule into the middle portion of the hair follicle.

b. Krause’s glands and Wolfring’s glands

Krause’s glands are small, mucous accessory lacrimal glands that are found underneath the eyelid where the upper and lower conjunctivae meet.

In the upper fornix, 20 to 40 glands of Krause are found, although only six to eight such glands appear in the lower fornix.

Its function is to produce tears which are secreted onto the surface of the conjunctiva. Wolfring’s glands are small tubular accessory lacrimal glands (glandulae lacrimales accessoriae) found in the lacrimal caruncle of the eyelid.

They are located in the upper border of the tarsus, approximately in the middle between the extremities of the tarsal glands. The glands of Wolfring are less numerous.

3. Meibomian glands

Also called tarsal glands. They are holocrine type exocrine glands, along the rims of the eyelid inside the tarsal plate. Approximately 30 to 40 meibomian glands are found in the upper lid and 20 to 30 in the lower lid.

x On eyelid eversion, the vertical rows of the meibomian glands can sometimes be seen as yellow streaks through the palpebral conjunctiva. They produce meibum, an oily substance that prevents evaporation of the eye’s tear film.

4. The conjunctival goblet cells.

a. Goblet cells are highly specialized epithelial cells, present in mucosal tissues along the body.

b. The main function of these cells is to produce and secrete mucins, which hydrate and lubricate mucosal surfaces.

c. Goblet cells are confined to the conjunctival epithelium under non-pathogenic conditions in the eye.

THE EXCRETORY SYSTEM OF THE LACRIMAL APPARATUS( NASOLACRIMAL DRAINAGE SYSTEM)

Some tear fluid is lost by evaporation and some by reabsorption through conjunctival tissue, but approximately 75% is passed through the nasolacrimal drainage system. The nasolacrimal drainage system consists of;

a. Lacrimal puncta

They are small oval openings situated on small elevations,one in each lid,6mm from the medial canthhus at the junction of ciliary and lacrimal part of the lid margin.

b. Lacrimal canaliculus

· Are 2 in number, upper and lower; they connect the lacrimal sac with the lacrimal punctum.

· It consists of a vertical (2mm)and horizontal portion(6-8mm).

· Usually, each canaliculus opens separately into the lacrimal sac (2.5mm below the apex).

· They occasionally join together to form a common canaliculus before opening into the lacrimal sac.

· A mucosal fold present at this point to prevent reflux of tear is called ”Valve of Rosenmuller”.

c. Lcrimal sac

· It is lodged in the lacrimal fossa,formed by the lacrimal bone and the frontal process of the maxilla.

· Portion of the lacrimal sac above the medical palpebral ligament is called fundus.

· Distended lacrimal sac measures 15 mm in length and 5-6mm in width.

· It is enclosed by lacrimal fascia.In between the lacrimal sac and the fascia lies a fine venus plexus.

d. Nasolacrimal duct

· It is the downward continuation of the lacrimal sac.

· It is 12-24mm in length and 4mm in diameter.

· It is lodged in a bony canal and passes backwards,laterally and downwards to open into the inferior meatus of the rose.

· Upper end of the nasolacrimal duct is the narrowest portion.Several mucosal valves are described within the nasolacrimal duct.All of them are simply mucous membrane fold without any valvular function expect the valve of Hasner.

· “Valve of Hasner” is located at the lower end of the nasolacrimal duct.
