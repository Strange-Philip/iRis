Cranial nerves are involved in head and neck function, and processes such as eating, speech, vision and facial expression. There are 12 pairs of cranial nerves that arise directly from the brain.

They pass through or into the cranial bones (thus cranial nerves) and are numbered I to XII roughly in order from top (rostral) to bottom (caudal). The first two nerves (olfactory and optic) arise from the cerebrum, whereas the remaining ten emerge from the brain stem.

Simplistically, each cranial nerve can be described as being sensory, motor or both. We are looking at the overview of cranial nerves because half of the cranial nerves (from 2 to 7) are involved in sight.

The names of the cranial nerves relate to their function and they are also numerically identified in roman numerals (I-XII). They are

The cranial nerves and the spinal nerves are considered components of the peripheral nervous system.

Difference between Cranial and Spinal Nerves

Cranial nerves and spinal nerves are two types of nerves of the peripheral nervous system. The key difference between cranial and spinal nerves is that the cranial nerves come from the brain while the spinal nerves come from the spinal cord. Another difference between cranial and spinal nerves is that there are 12 cranial nerve pairs while there are 31 spinal nerve pairs in mammals. In addition, we can also identify a difference between cranial and spinal nerves based on their functions. That is, the cranial nerves coordinate the activities associated with the head and neck whereas, the spinal nerves coordinate the activities associated with all the body parts, below the neck. The two nerves also differ in the way of numbering and naming.

THE CRANIAL NERVES AND THEIR FUNCTIONS

CN I: Olfactory Nerve

Clinical function: Smell

Type: Sensory

CN II: Optic Nerve

Clinical function: Vision

Type: Sensory

CN III: Oculomotor Nerve

Clinical function: Movements of eyeball: most orbital muscles. See also IV, VI (parasympathetic: ciliary muscle, accommodation of lens, etc.; iris muscle, pupilloconstriction)

Type: Motor

CN IV: Trochlear Nerve

Clinical function: Movements of eyeball: superior oblique muscle. See also III, VI

Type: Motor

CN V: Trigeminal- has 3 divisions

Va: Ophthalmic nerve: Sensation from eyeball, anterior scalp, upper face.

Vb: Maxillary nerve: Sensation from nasal cavity and sinuses, palate, mid face, maxillary teeth

Vc: Mandibular nerve: Both motor and sensory functions. Muscles of mastication, tensor tympani. Sensation from chin, temple, oral cavity, tongue, temporomandibular joint (TMJ), mandibular teeth, ear, proprioception from muscles of mastication.

The trigeminal nerve is responsible for sensation. Both ophthalmic and maxillary nerves are sensory but mandibular nerve is mixed.

CN VI: Abducens Nerve

Clinical function: Movements of eyeball: lateral rectus muscle. See also III, IV

Type: Motor

CN VII: Facial Nerve

Clinical Function: Muscles of facial expression, stapedius (middle ear) (parasympathetic: lacrimal, nasal, palatine, submandibular, sublingual glands) (taste: anterior tongue)

Type: Mixed (both sensory and motor)

CN VIII: Vestibulocochlear Nerve

Clinical function: Hearing, balance

Type: Sensory

CN IX: Glossopharyngeal Nerve

Clinical Function: Sensation from oropharynx, posterior tongue, carotid body and sinus (taste: posterior tongue) (muscle: stylopharyngeus) (parasympathetic: parotid gland)

Type: Mixed

CN X: Vagus Nerve

Clinical Function: Muscles of larynx, pharynx (phonation, swallowing) Sensation from larynx, hypopharynx, heart, lungs, abdominal viscera (taste: epiglottic region, hypopharynx) (parasympathetic: cardiac muscle; muscles and glands of foregut and midgut: intestinal activity)

Type: Mixed

CN XI: Spinal Accessory Nerve

Function: Muscles: sternocleidomastoid, trapezius

Type: Motor

CN XII Hypoglossal Nerve

Function: Tongue muscles and movements

Type: Motor

NB: The largest of the cranial nerves is the trigeminal nerve, which is the cranial nerve 5. It has three divisions containing both sensory and motor nerves. The trochlear nerve is the smallest of the cranial nerves. It is the fourth cranial nerve (CN IV) and one of the ocular motor nerves that controls eye movement.

CRANIAL NERVES INVOLVED IN VISION

The orbital structures are innervated by cranial nerves (CNs) II, III, IV, V, VI, and VII. Motor functions of the striated muscles are controlled by CN III, the oculomotor nerve; CN IV, the trochlear nerve; CN VI, the abducens nerve; and CN VII, the facial nerve. CN V, the trigeminal nerve, carries the sensory supply from the orbital structures. CN II, the optic nerve, carries visual information.

NB: The trigeminal nerve is the main sensory nerve involved in vision. Its 3 divisions also have sub-divisions.

The majority of the nerves directed to the eye are composed by thin, unmyelinated nerve fibers often incompletely surrounded by Schwann cells, while less than 30 percent present a myelin sheath. The absence of myelin sheath increases transparency especially in refracting surfaces.

The sensory innervation of the eye is provided by the peripheral axons of primary sensory neurons located in the trigeminal ganglion. The sensory nerves enter the eyeball mainly through the ciliary nerves and reach all ocular tissues with the exception of the lens and the retina.

Ocular innervation is particularly rich in the cornea but all tissues of the anterior segment of the eye have an abundant sensory supply.

Ocular sensory fibers are functionally heterogeneous and include mechanoreceptor, polymodal nociceptor, and cold receptor types of sensory units.

Consequently, the nerves respond to a variety of physical and chemical stimuli. Impulse activity originated at peripheral sensory nerve fibers of the eye reaches second-order neurons located at the trigeminal nucleus caudalis in the lower brainstem. Sensory information travels subsequently to relay stations in the contralateral posterior thalamic nucleus and finally reaches the cortex.
