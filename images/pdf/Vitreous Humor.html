WHAT IS VITREOUS HUMOUR?

Vitreous is a transparent colorless, gel-like acellular connective tissue that occupies the posterior compartment of the eye. The word,” vitreous” is derived from a Latin word, “vitrum” which means glass. This is because the vitreous has a glass-like appearance.

VOLUME: It makes up 80% of the volume of the eye. IE, the largest structure of the eye.

REFRACTIVE INDEX: 1.336

WEIGHT: Approximately 4g

pH: 7.0-7.4

BOUNDARY

· Anteriorly – posterior surface of the crystalline lens

  • Peripherally – Pars plana of the ciliary body
  • Posteriorly – Retina and optic disc

COMPOSITION OF VITREOUS

A. Vitreous is composed of about 99% water.

B. Remaining 1% is made up of:

1. Collagen fibrils. ( Type II collagen is the most abundant, about 90% and the remaining being type IX)

-Helps for vitreous attachments

2. Hyaluronic acid.

- Maintains the vitreous structure. IE, the jelly-like nature of the vitreous.

3. Salts

4. Soluble protein

NOTE : Irrespective of its high water composition (99%), it is describe as gel-like because of the presence of type II collagen. The breakdown of type II collagen as a result of old age leads to loss of its gel-like nature.

In humans, vitreous humour begins as fluid (20%) and gel (80%). Gradually changes to fluid (60%) and gel (40%).

DEVELOPMENT OF VITREOUS

The development of vitreous is divided into 3 stages. They are:

A. Primary vitreous

B. Secondary vitreous

C. Tertiary vitreous

A. PRIMARY VITREOUS

i. Develops at the end of 3rd embryonic week

ii. Contains primary hyaloid vessels. These are equivalent to blood vessels.

iii. The hyaloid vessels dissolves before birth.

iv. After the hyaloid vessels have dissolved, the passage way through which they travelled remains as a canal known as the, “cloquent’s canal” or “hyaloid canal”

B. SECONDARY VITREOUS

i. Starts to develop by the 9th week.

ii. This becomes the mature vitreous. IE, secondary vitreous eventually fills the globe and encircles and dissolves the primary vitreous.

iii. It is mostly acellular and fibrous.

C. TERTIARY VITREOUS

i. Begins to develop at 6month of embryonic stage

ii. It is the condensed fibrous structure of the secondary vitreous. This eventually forms the zonules.

iii. The zonules merge with the lens capsule and the ciliary body.

VITREOUS ATTACHMENTS

Vitreous forms several attachments with the surrounding structures by the aid of the collagen fibrils.

Below are some vitreous attachments arranged in decreasing order of strengths:

1. The strongest attachment is the vitreous base – at ora serrata (NB- Ora serrata is the place where retina terminates)

2. Attachment at the posterior lens

- This attachment is made possible by the hyaloideoscapsular ligament (of Weiger) or retrolental ligament.

- Also, the retrolental space (of Berger) is a potential space found within the ring formed by the retrolental ligament. This potential space is present because the lens and vitreous are juxtaposed.

3. Attachment with optic disc

4. Attachment with macula

5. The weakest attachment is with the blood vessels.

FUNCTIONS OF THE VITREOUS

1. Serves as a storage area – for metabolites for the retina and lens

2. Protection – because of its viscoelastic properties, it act as a shock absorber protecting the fragile retinal tissue during rapid eye movements and strenuous physical activity

3. Provides a clear medium for light transmission.

QUESTION

Why was it imperative for hyaloid vessels to dissolve?

ANSWER

To enhance the transparency property of vitreous.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE

1. VITREOUS HAEMORRHAGE

- This condition occurs when some retinal blood vessels rupture and bleed into the vitreous humour. The presence of blood reduces the transparency of the vitreous and can serve as an obstacle to light focusing on the retinal.

2. POSTERIOR VITREAL DETACHMENT(PVD)

PVD is a natural change that occurs during adulthood, when the vitreous separates from the retina.

3. PERIPHERAL RETINAL FRACTION

With aging, the vitreous base adhesion extend further posteriorly, and the border approaches the equator. These changes can increase traction on the peripheral retina and might contribute to retinal tears and detachment.

4. FLOATERS

A disruption in the HA-collagen complex causes collagen fibrils to aggregate to bundles which may become large enough to be visible.
