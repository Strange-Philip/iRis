OVERVIEW:

Eyelids are moveable skin folds of tissues that cover the front of the orbit. The eyelid is one of the ocular adnexa. The eyelid has the thinnest skin in the body. It has structures that produce tear film.

The eyelids are important for protection and maintenance of normal ocular health and function.

1. Barrier function

• Eyelid closure provides a barrier function elicited by voluntary or reflexive Blinking.

2. Maintenance of globe position

• The eyelids apply gentle posterior pressure on the globe to counteract forward pressure from orbital tissues behind the globe.

3. Ocular surface integrity.

• Blinking distributes tears across the ocular surface and promotes drainage of tears via the lacrimal pump mechanism.

4. Eyelid glands

• The eyelid contains glands with secretions that add to the tear film.

EYEBROWS

Eyebrows consists of thick skin covered by characteristic, short, prominent hairs extending across the superior orbital margin. Eyebrows are the first body hairs produced during embryologic development.

Eyebrow position and contour are maintained by a group of muscles innervated by the temporal branch of the facial nerve (CN VII).

The muscles of the eyebrow and forehead may be divided into

a. Superficial (frontalis, procerus, orbicularis oculi),

b. Intermediate (depressor supercilii)

c. Deep (corrugator supercilii)

FM: frontalis muscle OM: orbicularis muscle CSM: corrugated superciilli muscle PM: Procerus muscle DSM: Depressor supercilli muscle

Arrangement:

a. Males: The brows run along orbital margin.

b. Females: the brows run above the orbital margin.

Eyebrow redirects sweat away from the eyes. The skin of the eyebrows and its subcutaneous fibroadipose tissue are the thickest layers on the face.

• It plays an important role in facial expression

Movement:

There are 4 muscles that produce the eyebrow and facial movements.

a. Frontalis: raises the eyebrow, causing a look of surprise or attention.

b. Corrugator superciliasis: it moves the brow medially, toward the nose, creating vertical furrows between the brows.

c. Procerus: muscle of menace or aggression, pulls the medial portion of the eyebrow inferiorly and produces horizontal furrows over the bridge of the nose.

d. The orbicularis oculi: lowers the entire brow.

EYELID

The eyelid, AKA palpebrae, are folds of skin that cover the globe when closed. The space between the upper and lower eyelids that exposes the eyeball is called palpebral fissure.

The upper eyelid is more movable than the lower and contains in its superior margin, the levator palpebral superioris muscle.

Dimensions

• In adults, the normal interpalpebral fissure height is 8–11 mm; the horizontal palpebral fissure length is 27–30 mm.

• The upper lid margin rests 1.5–2 mm below the limbus; the lower rests on the Limbus.

The eye lid is composed of skin (epidermis and dermis), eyelid margin sub-cutaneous tissue, the orbicularis oculi, Orbital septum, levator muscle, MÜller muscle, Tarsal plates, tarsal glands, and the conjunctiva.

EYELID MARGIN:

The eyelid margin may be divided into anterior and posterior lamella.

The anterior lamella is composed of

- skin, muscle, and associated glands (Zeiss and Moll),

The posterior lamella consists of

- the tarsal plate, conjunctiva, and associated glands (Meibomian).

The two lamellae may be separated from one another along the gray line (intermaginal sulcus).

A terminal extension of the orbicularis known as the muscle of Riolan keeps the eyelid edges close to the surface of the globe as well as help expel glandular contents during blinking.

Eyelid closure

• Reflex blinking provides rapid closure of the eye in response to splash or foreign body sensation.

Bell’s phenomenon

• A normal Bell’s phenomenon provides involuntary upward rotation of the globe on lid closure, removing the cornea from noxious stimuli. Clinical Notes: Bell’s palsy

Structure

1. Anterior lamella:

The anterior lamella functions as a single unit, consisting of skin, muscle (orbicularis oculi), and associated glands (Glands of Zeiss and Moll)

(i) Skin

• The eyelid skin is thinnest, allowing rapid and large movements on eyelid opening and closure. The eyelid skin is the thinnest in the body, due to relative thinning of dermis and absence of a subcutaneous fat layer.

It contains; fine hairs (eyelashes) 2-3 irregular rows. Upper eyelashes are longer and more; the sebaceous and sweat glands.

Eyelashes:

- Are sensitive to touch (protects the eye)

- Elicit the blink reflex

(ii) Muscle: the orbicularis oculi

• The orbicularis oculi (OO) are a flat, elliptical muscle surrounding the orbital margin and extending onto the cheek, eyelids, and around the lacrimal sac. The major protractor of the eyelids is the orbicularis muscle.

It is skeletal and has varying short myofibers. The variability in myofiber length may help to explain the variety of complex eyelid movements including blinking, winking, and spasm.

The short muscle fibers connected by myomyous junctions. It is the thinnest muscle fibers of the face.

Innervated by the facial nerve.

• OO has three functional divisions;

- Orbital: forced eyelid closure

- Preseptal: voluntary eyelid closure, regulates lacrimal pucta

- Pretarsal: spontaneous blinking

• Contraction of the OO on blinking aids the lacrimal pump

• The muscle of Riolan, the pretarsal portion of OO adjacent to the lid margin, helps rotate the lashes out during lid closure and releases secretions from Meibomian gland.

(iii) Glands

• The glands of Zeiss (modified sebaceous glands) and Moll (modified sweat glands) are found in the anterior lamellae near the eyelash cilia. Both secrete their contents around the lash follicle.

(iv) Cilia

• Cilia are modified hairs found on eyelid and lid margin skin that protect the eye from large airborne particles.

• There are 100–150 on the upper lid and 75 on the lower lid and are replaced every 3–5 months.

• Cilia are sensory organs; stimulation results in reflex blinking.

2. Posterior lamella

The posterior lamella is composed of tarsal plate, conjunctiva, and associated Glands (tarsal glands).

(i) The tarsal plates

Tarsal plates are composed of collagen fibers (types I and III collagen) as well as components more typical of cartilage (aggrecan, chondroitins 4 and 6 sulfate).

Function: They provide both rigidity and allow for dynamic movement of the eyelids. It also provides strength and protection.

(ii) Glands

The tarsal plates of both the upper and lower eyelids contain the sebaceous meibomian glands. There are approximately 25 in the upper eyelid and 20 in the lower eyelid.

Function: They secrete the lipid layer (outer layer) of the tear film which helps prevents evaporation of the tears.

The accessory glands are found in the conjunctiva.

Clinical NB:

In the setting of meibomian gland dysfunction, the relative absence of the lipid layer may result in dry eyes. Chronic inflammation of the glands or meibomitis, may lead to

- Dysplastic hair follicles that curve towards the globe. It could be as a result of;

1. distichiasis (misdirected lashes from the meibomian gland orifices) or

2. trichiasis (misdirection of normal eyelash follicles

- Treatment: may include

cryotherapy, argon laser, electrolysis, trephination and other lid-modifying modalities

(iii) Conjunctiva

Tightly bound to the posterior aspect of the tarsal plates is the palpebral conjunctiva. It consists of a non-keratinized stratified columnar epithelium overlying loose connective tissue known as substantia propria.

The accessory glands of

Wolfring : located in the tarsal conjunctiva, and

Krause: located in conjunctival fornix,

Function: Both help to produce the aqueous layer of the tear film.

This middle layer of the tear film is tightly bound to the hydrophilic mucin layer (inner layer) produced from conjunctival goblet cells.

Eyelid Movements

1. Opening

Eyelid movements are linked to gaze, such that the eyelids move up on upward gaze and vice versa.

• Contracture of the levator palpebrae superioris muscle (innervated by the oculomotor nerve) elevates the upper eyelid approximately 15 mm

• Muller’s muscle (smooth muscle, sympathetically innervated) contributes an additional 1–2 mm of upper lid elevation.

• The lower lid is moved inferiorly (5 mm) by the inferior retractors linked to the inferior rectus and inferior oblique by the capsulopalpebral fascia.

2. Closure

• Closure is due primarily to OO contraction; additionally there is simultaneous levator palpaebrae superioris relaxation.

3. Eyelid motor control

• Eyelid opening and closure is controlled in the frontal cortex close to the oculogyric centers.

• The caudal central nucleus of the oculomotor complex in the midbrain supplies the levator palpebrae superioris.

• Both eyelids obey Hering’s law: they are linked as yolk muscles and bilaterally.

Blinking

Blinking can be spontaneous, reflex, or voluntary.

Blinking assists in the maintenance of a normal ocular tear surface. A blink initiates a cycle of secretion, dispersal, evaporation, and drainage of tears.

• Blinking results from simultaneous:

(a) Contraction of the eyelid protractors (orbicularis oculi, corrugator, and procerus muscles)

(b) Relaxation of the eyelid retractors (levator palpebrae superioris and frontalis muscles)

1. Spontaneous blinking

• This occurs every 3–8 s, lasting 0.3–0.4 s with a blink rate of 12bpm.

• The spontaneous blink rate is affected by:

(a) Environment (dry, moist, dust, bright)

(b) Emotional state (anxiety, concentration)

(c) Some disease states (e.g., Parkinson’s disease)

NB: The pretarsal portion of the orbicularis muscle is thought to be responsible for spontaneous blinking.

2. Reflex blinking

• Reflex blinking occurs rapidly in response to the following stimulus types:

(a) Tactile: corneal, eyelash, eyelid skin, and eyebrow contact

(b) Optical: dazzle (bright lights), menace (unexpected or threatening

objects)

(c) Auditory (menace)

• The tactile blinking reflex is served by a simple neural circuit consisting of the trigeminal nerve (afferent arm) and facial nerve (efferent arm).

• It can be modified by supranuclear influences.

• The dazzle reflex is mediated at a subcortical level via the supraoptic nucleus and superior colliculus, while the menace reflex mediated at a cortical level.

• The afferent information for both reflexes is transmitted via the optic nerve.

3. Voluntary blinking

• The amplitude of voluntary blinking is usually larger than reflex and spontaneous blinking as all three divisions of OO may be used
