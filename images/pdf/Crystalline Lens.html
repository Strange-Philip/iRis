The crystalline lens is a biconvex, avascular and a transparent elliptic structure that aids in focusing light rays on the retina.

Structure of lens

The lens has an anterior and a posterior surface with the posterior surface being more curved. The central elevation of both surfaces is anterior and posterior poles respectively.

How the lens receives nourishment

Since the lens is avascular, it receives nourishment from the aqueous humour.

Position of the lens

The lens is located within posterior chamber, anterior to the posterior chamber and posterior to the iris. It is suspended behind the iris by the zonules which connects it to the ciliary body.

Transparency of the lens

Factors that contributes to the transparency of lens includes:

1. Crystallins

2. Absence of membrane-bound organelles in fibers

1. Crystallins are water-soluble proteins that compose over 90% of the protein within the lens. The three main crystallins types found in the human eye are α-, β-, and γ-crystallins. Crystallins tend to form soluble, high-molecular weight aggregates that pack tightly in lens fibers, thus increasing the index of refraction of the lens while maintaining its transparency. β and γ crystallins are found primarily in the lens, while subunits of α -crystallin have been isolated from other parts of the eye and the body. α-crystallin proteins belong to a larger superfamily of molecular chaperone proteins, and so it is believed that the crystallin proteins were evolutionarily recruited from chaperone proteins for optical purposes. The chaperone functions of α-crystallin may also help maintain the lens proteins, which must last a human for their entire lifetime.

2. Another important factor in maintaining the transparency of the lens is the absence of light-scattering organelles such as the nucleus, endoplasmic reticulum, and mitochondria within the mature lens fibers. Lens fibers also have a very extensive cytoskeleton that maintains the precise shape and packing of the lens fibers; disruptions/mutations in certain cytoskeletal elements can lead to the loss of transparency.

DEVELOPMENT OR EMBRYOLOGY OF LENS

1. Cells that form the lens are originally part of the surface ectoderm covering the head of the embryo.

2. Neural epithelial cells in the embryonic forebrain bulge laterally to form the optic vesicles, which eventually contact the surface ectoderm cells.

3. After they make contact, the optic vesicles and the prospective lens ectoderm cells secretes an extracellular matrix that causes these cell layers to adhere tightly to each other

4. The prospective lens cells elongate to form the thickened lens placode.

5. The lens placode and the outer surface of the optic vesicle invaginate to form the lens pit or pore and optic cup respectively.

6. The invaginated lens placode soon separate from the surface ectoderm by a process involving the death of the cells in the connecting stalk between the cells of the surface ectoderm and the lens placode.

7. During invagination, the optic vesicle and lens separates because extracellular matrix between them diminishes.

8. The epithelial cells that gives rise to the lens vesicle lie on a thin basal membrane. During invagination, this thin lamina comes to surround the lens vesicle. It gradually thickens by the deposition of the successive layers of basal lamina material to form the lens capsule.

9. The cells in the posterior end elongates and soon obliterate the lumen of the vesicle. These cells are then known as primary fiber cells.

10. Primary fiber cells formation establishes the fundamental structure of the lens with epithelial cells covering the anterior surface and elongate fiber cells filling the bulk of the lens.

A. Lens capsule

The lens capsule is an elastic transparent envelope that surrounds the entire lens .Although the lens is elastic in nature in nature but it has no elastic fibers. It is highly elastic because of the lamella arrangement of the fibers. It elastic nature causes its shape to be altered by the pull the zonules or suspensory ligaments.

It thickness varies with location, being thinnest at the poles and equator. It thickest in an annular area around the anterior pole. The capsule is actually a basement membrane of the lens epithelium.

The capsule consist primarily of collagen. . Specifically the type IV collagen. Other components of the capsule includes, laminin, entadin (nidogen), and heparin sulfate proteoglycan

The outer superficial zone of the capsule is called the zonular lamella. The anterior lens capsule

Function of the capsule

1. Encloses all lens components

2. Helps to mold the shape of the lens.

3. Provides some barrier functions, preventing large molecules such as albumin and hemoglobin from entering the lens.

LENS EPITHELIUM

Lens epithelium, located in the anterior portion of the lens between the capsule and the lens fibers is a simple cuboidal epithelium. No posterior epithelium is present because it was used during embryonic development to form the primary lens fibers.

Functions of cells of the lens epithelium.

1. Secretes the anterior capsule throughout life.

2. Site of metabolic transport mechanisms.

- The lens undergoes glucose metabolism. This serves as the main energy source of the lens. Glycolysis about 80% of the lens energy because the lens is in an anaerobic environment and since glycosis is an anaerobic glucose metabolism method.

- Progenitors for new fibers

The basal aspect of the lens epithelium is adjacent to the capsule and the apical portion is oriented inward toward the center of the lens.

The lateral membrane of the epithelial cells are joined by desmosomes and gab junctions.

LENS FIBER

Cells near the equatorial margin of the lens epithelium in the region called the germinative zone, proliferate slowly. Most of the cells produced by mitosis in this region migrate towards the posterior of the lens and differentiate into fiber cells at the lens equator.

These new fiber cells elongates and accumulate large amount of crystallins. During elongation, the posterior (basal) end of the fiber cells move along the inner surface of the capsule and their anterior (apical) ends slide beneath the epithelium until they meet the elongating cells from the other side of the lens near the posterior and anterior midlines.

The junction between the ends of the cells from the opposite sides of the lens are called sutures. Once fiber cells reach the sutures, they stop elongating and their basal end detaches from the capsule.

Soon after reaching the sutures, the fiber cells degrade all intracellular membrane-bound organelles including their nuclei, mitochondria and endoplasmic reticulum.

The lens fibers form the bulk of the lens. They are long, thin, transparent, firmly packed with diameter typically between 4-7micrometers and length up to 12mm long. They stretch lengthwise from the posterior to the anterior poles.

When cut horizontally, they arranged in concentric layers rather like layer of an onion. If cut along the equator, it appears as a honeycomb.

The lens fibers are linked via gab junctions and interdigitations of the cells that resemble, “ball and socket” joints.

The lens is split into regions depending on the age of the lens fiber of a particular layer. Moving outward from the central, oldest layer, the lens is split into:

1. An embryonic nucleus

2. The fetal nucleus

3. The adult nucleus

4. And the outer cortex.

New lens fibers generated from lens epithelium, are added to the outer cortex.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE

  1. Cataracts

Cataracts are opacities of the lens. While some are small and do not require any treatment, others may be large enough to block light and obstruct vision. Cataracts usually develop as the aging lens becomes more and more opaque, but cataracts can also form congenitally or after injury to the lens. Nuclear sclerosis is a type of age-related cataract. Diabetes is another risk factor for cataract. Cataract surgery involves the removal of the lens and insertion of an artificial intraocular lens.

  1. Presbyopia

Presbyopia is the age-related loss of accommodation, which is marked by the inability of the eye to focus on nearby objects. The exact mechanism is still unknown, but age-related changes in the hardness, shape, and size of the lens have all been linked to the condition.

  1. Ectopia lentis

Ectopia Lentis is the displacement of the lens from its normal position.

  1. Aphakia

Aphakia is the absence of the lens from the eye. Aphakia can be the result of surgery or injury, or it can be congenital.
