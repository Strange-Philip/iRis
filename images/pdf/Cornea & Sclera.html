THE CORNEA

An Overview of the Cornea

Thin the centre but thick at the periphery Concave but aqueous neutralizes and gives it the positive

  • The cornea is the principal refracting component of the eye.

· Its transparency and avascularity provide optimal light transmittance.

· The anterior surface of the cornea is covered by the tear film, and the posterior surface borders the aqueous-filled anterior chamber.

· At its periphery, the cornea is continuous with the conjunctiva and the sclera.

· From anterior to posterior, the five layers that compose the cornea are epithelium, Bowman’s layer, stroma, Descemet’s membrane, and endothelium.

1. Corneal Dimensions (Figure 2)

· The transparent cornea appears from the front to be oval, as the sclera encroaches on the superior and inferior aspects.

· The anterior horizontal diameter is 12 mm and the anterior vertical diameter is 11 mm.

· If viewed from behind, the cornea appears circular, with horizontal and vertical diameters of 11.7 mm.

· In profile, the cornea has an elliptic rather than a spherical shape, the curvature being steeper in the center and flatter near the periphery.

· The radius of curvature of the central cornea at the anterior surface is 7.8 mm and
at the posterior surface is 6.5 mm.

· The central corneal thickness is 0.53 mm, whereas the corneal periphery is 0.71 mm thick (All values given are approximations.)

Figure 4: The Epithelium Layer

§ The outermost layer of stratified corneal epithelium.

· It is five to seven cells thick and measures approximately 50 μm.

· The epithelium thickens in the periphery and is continuous with the conjunctival epithelium at the limbus.

· The surface layer of corneal epithelium is two cells thick and displays a very smooth anterior surface.

  • It consists of nonkeratinized squamous cells, each of which contains a flattened nucleus and fewer cellular organelles than deeper cells.

· Cell size varies but a superficial cell can be 50 μm in diameter and 5 μm in height.

· The plasma membrane of the surface epithelial cells secretes a glycocalyx component that adjoins the mucin layer of the tear film

Functions of the Epithelium Layer

· It enhances tear film stability

ü As many projections (microplicae; the fingerlike projection and microvilli; the ridiglike projection) located on the apical surfaces of outermost cell increase surface area, thus enhance tear film stability.

· Prevent uptake of excess fluid from the tears (Figure 5)

ü Tight junctions (zonula occludens) join the surface cells along the lateral wall near the apical surfaces. These junctures provide a barrier to intercellular movement of substances from the tear layer and prevent the
uptake of excess fluid from the tear film.

  • Quick regeneration

ü Maintenance of a smooth corneal surface depends on replacement of surface cells.

ü Cell proliferation occurs in the basal layer,

ü Only cells in contact with basement membrane have the ability to divide

ü Stem cells located in a 0.5-1mm wide band around the corneal periphery are responsible for renewal of the basal cell layer

ü A slow migration of cells occurs from the periphery to the centre of the cornea

ü Turn over time for the entire epithelium is 7 days. However, if the basement membrane is damaged, complete healing takes several months.

Figure 5: The function of Epithelium Layer

§ The middle layer of the corneal epithelium

ü Is made up of two to three layers of wing cells.

ü These cells have wing like lateral processes, are polyhedral, and
have convex anterior surfaces and concave posterior surfaces that fit over the basal cells.

ü The diameter of a wing cell is approximately 20 μm.

ü Desmosomes and gap junctions join wing cells to each other, and desmosomes join wing cells to surface and basal cells.

§ The innermost basal cell layer of the corneal epithelium

ü Is a single layer of columnar cells, with diameters ranging from 8 to 10 μm.

ü These cells contain oval-shaped nuclei displaced toward the apex and
oriented at right angles to the surface.

ü The rounded, apical surface of each cell lies adjacent to the wing cells,
and the basal surface attaches to the underlying basement membrane (basal lamina).

ü The basal cells secrete this basement membrane, which attaches the cells to the underlying tissue through hemidesmosomes.

v The Bowman’s Layer

· It is the second layer of the cornea

  • Approximately 8-14μm thick

· Dense fibrous sheet of inter-woven collagen fibrils randomly arranged in a mucoprotein ground substance.

· Layer commonly referred to as a “membrane” but more correctly is a transition layer to the stroma.

Figure 6: The Bowman’s Layer

· It is acellular and contains collagen fibrils of lesser diameter than those in the stroma.

· It is produced prenatally by the epithelium and is not believed to regenerate due to absence of fibroblasts

· If injured, the layer is replaced by epithelial cells or stromal scar tissue.

· The layer is very resistant to damage by shearing, penetration, or infection.

v Stroma/Substantia Propria

Figure 8:

· It is approximately 500μm thick, or about 90% of the corneal thickness.

  • It is composed of:

Ø collagen fibrils

Ø keratocytes

Ø extracellular ground substance.

· The fibrils have a uniform 25-35nm diameter and run parallel to each other, forming flat bundles called lamellae.

· The 200 to 300 lamellae are distributed throughout the stroma and lie parallel to the corneal surface.

· Keratocytes (corneal fibroblasts) are flattened cells that lie between and occasionally within the lamellae.

· They maintain the stroma by synthesizing collagen and extracellular matrix components.

· Other cells may be found between lamellae including white blood cells, lymphocytes, macrophages, and polymorphonuclear leucocytes.

· These increase in number in pathological conditions.

· Ground substance fills the areas between:

Ø Fibrils

Ø Lamellae

Ø Cells

· It contains proteoglycans, a core protein with an anionic polysaccharide side chain called glycosaminoglycan (GAG) portion.

  • Proteoglycans

Ø Provide tissue volume

Ø Maintain spatial order of collagen fibrils

Ø Resist compressive forces

Ø Gives viscoelastic properties to the tissue

· GAG’s attract and bind with water, maintaining the precise spatial relationship between individual fibrils.

· Dermatan sulphate is found in oxygen rich environments (anterior cornea)

· Keratan is found in low oxygen environments (posterior cornea)

· The very regular arrangement of the stromal components and the small diameter of collagen fibrils contribute to the stromal transparency.

· The very specific spacing between fibrils allows destructive interference of rays reflected from adjacent fibrils.

· Overall, the cornea scatters less than 1% of light that enters it.

v Descemet’s Membrane

Descemet’s membrane is considered the basement membrane of the endothelium. It is produced continually and therefore thickens throughout life, such that it has doubled by age 40 years. In children it is 5μm thick and will increase to approximately 15μm over a lifetime.

· Descemet’s membrane consists of two laminae:

Ø The anterior lamina: it is approximately 3μm thick, exhibits a banded appearance and is a latticework of collagen fibrils secreted during embryonic development.

Ø The posterior lamina: it is non-banded and homogeneous; it is the portion secreted by the endothelium throughout life.

Ø The collagen fibrils are arranged such that Descemet's membrane exhibits an elastic property

Ø If injured, the membrane will curl into the anterior chamber.

Ø It is very resistant to trauma, proteolytic enzymes, and some pathological conditions and can be regenerated when damaged.

Ø A thickened area of collagenous connective tissue may be seen at the membrane’s
termination in the limbus; this circular structure is called Schwalbe’s line (or ring).

  • Regular arrangement of the cells is referred to as the endothelial mosaic.

3. Corneal Function

Ø The cornea has two primary functions:

ü to refract light

ü to transmit light: The cornea transmits 99% of incident light this is due to its high level of transparency

· Factors that affect the amount of Corneal Refraction

Ø The curvature of the anterior corneal surface

Ø The change in refractive index from air to cornea (actually the tear film)

Ø Corneal thickness

Ø The curvature of the posterior corneal surface

Ø The change in refractive index from cornea
to aqueous humor.

· The total refractive power of the eye focused at infinity is between 60 and 65 diopters (D), with 43 to 48 D attributable to the cornea.

· Absorption of Ultraviolet Radiation (UVR) by Corneal in the Transmission of Light

Ø The cornea transmits light with wavelengths between
310 and 2500 nm.

Ø Wave lengths below 300 nm are absorbed by the epithelium and Bowman’s layer and do not penetrate deeper; those between 300 to 320 nm are absorbed by corneal stroma.

Ø The ability of the cornea to absorb the shorter wavelengths of ultraviolet radiation is protective to deeper structures (lens and retina), but the cornea is vulnerable to damage from this constant exposure.

Ø UVR induces oxidative stress by generating reactive oxygen species (ROS).

Ø These free radicals are highly reactive because of an unpaired electron and can damage cellular structures.

Ø The corneal epithelium has some protection against the damage caused by UVR absorption.

Ø Its cells have high concentrations of ascorbate (vitamin C) and glutathione.

Ø Ascorbate can absorb UVR, and is also a cellular antioxidant that can reduce free radicals and neutralize their activity.

Ø Glutathione is both a reducing agent and a free radical scavenger.

4. The Transparency of the Cornea

Ø Avascular: The cornea is avascular and obtains its nourishment by diffusion from the aqueous humor and from the conjunctival and episcleral capillary networks located in the limbus.

Ø Regular diameter of fibres

5. The Limbus

  • The limbus, located at the corneoscleral junction, is a band approximately 1.5 to 2 mm wide that encircles the periphery of the cornea.

· The radius of curvature abruptly changes at this junction of cornea and sclera, creating a narrow furrow, the external scleral sulcus.

  • Internally at this juncture, there is a larger furrow, the internal scleral sulcus, which has a scooped-out appearance and contains the trabecular meshwork and
    the canal of Schlemm, the major route for drainage of the aqueous humor.

  • Histologically, the anterior boundary of the limbus consists of a plane connecting the termination of Bowman’s layer and the termination of Descemet’s membrane.

  • The posterior boundary is a plane perpendicular to the surface of the globe and passing through the posterior edge of the scleral spur.

· These boundaries are used in this discussion, although clinically the boundaries are not as definitive.

· The limbus is the transitional zone between cornea and conjunctiva, and between cornea and sclera.

· Some layers of the cornea continue into the limbal area and others terminate.

  • In the limbus;

Ø the very regular squamous corneal epithelium becomes the thicker columnar conjunctival epithelium

Ø the very regular corneal stroma becomes the irregularly arranged scleral stroma

Ø the corneal endothelial sheet becomes discontinuous to wrap around the
strands of the trabecular meshwork

Ø Bowman’s layer and Descemet’s membrane terminate at the anterior border

Ø the conjunctival stroma, episcleral, and Tenon’s capsule begin within the limbal area.

THE SCLERA

An Overview of the Sclera

The sclera forms the posterior five-sixths of the connective tissue coat of the globe. The sclera maintains the shape of the globe, offering resistance to internal and external forces, and provides an attachment for the extraocular muscle insertions. The thickness of the sclera varies from 1 mm at the posterior pole to 0.3 mm just behind the rectus muscle insertions.

1. Layers of the Sclera

· There are basically two layers of the sclera. They are;

Ø Sclera propa: is the innermost aspect of the sclera merges with the choroidal tissue in the suprachoroid layer and it is avascular.

Ø Episclera: is a loose, vascularised, connective tissue that lies just outer to the sclera. The larger episcleral blood vessels are visible through the conjunctiva. The episclera is joined to the Tenon’s capsule by strands of connective tissue and progressively becomes thinner towards the back of the eye.

· The sclera itself is continuous with the corneal stroma at the limbus.

· The diameter of collagen fibrils varies from 25 to 230nm.

· The fibrils are arranged in irregular bundles that branch and interlace.

· Bundle widths and thickness vary

· External bundles are narrower and thinner than deeper bundles.

2. SCLERA

· Orientation of bundles in sclera is very irregular compared to that in the cornea.

· Bundles in outer regions run approximately parallel to the surface with interweaving between the bundles

· In inner regions bundles run in all directions.

· This random arrangement and the vast amount of interweaving may give strength and flexibility to the eyeball.

· Elastic fibers are sometimes found in the sclera between and sometimes within bundles.

· Fibroblasts are also present though less numerous than in the cornea

· Stromal ground substance is similar to corneal ground substance but with fewer glycosaminoglycan (GAG’s).

· Innermost aspect of the sclera merges with the choroid in the suprachoroid layer.

3. The Scleral Spur

The scleral spur is a region of circularly oriented collagen bundles that extends from the inner aspect of the sclera. In its entirety, the scleral spur is actually a ring, although on cross section it appears wedge shaped, resembling a spur.

At the spur’s posterior edge, its fibers blend with the more obliquely arranged scleral fibers. The posterior scleral spur is the origin of the longitudinal ciliary muscle fibers and most of the trabecular meshwork sheets attach to its anterior aspect, such that the collagen of the spur is continuous with that of the trabeculae

4. Sclera Opacity

  • Scleral opacity depends on:

Ø Number of GAG’s

Ø Amount of water

Ø Size and distribution of collagen fibrils.

· Sclera contain ¼ the amount of GAG’s found in the cornea.

· Hence the sclera is less hydrated than the cornea (68%)

· The greater variation in fibril size and irregular spacing between the components induce light scattering

· This renders the sclera opaque.

5. Scleral Color

The anterior sclera is visible through the conjunctiva and, if healthy, is white but may appear colored as a result of age or disease. In the newborn, the sclera has a bluish tint because it is almost transparent and the underlying vascular uvea shows through. The sclera also may appear blue in connective tissue diseases that cause scleral thinning. The sclera might appear yellow in the presence of fatty deposits, which can occur with age. Likewise, the sclera may appear yellow
in liver disease because of the buildup of metabolic wastes.

6. Scleral Foramina and Canal

The sclera contains a number of foramina and canals. The anterior scleral foramen is the area occupied by the cornea. The optic nerve passes through the posterior scleral foramen, which is bridged by a network of scleral tissue called the lamina cribrosa. It is similar to a sieve, with interwoven collagen fibrils forming canals through which the optic nerve bundles pass.

The lamina cribrosa is the weakest area of the outer connective tissue tunic. The canals that pass through the sclera carry nerves and vessels, and are possible routes by which disease can exit or enter the eye. The canals are designated by their location.

The posterior apertures are located around the posterior scleral foramen and are the passages for the posterior ciliary arteries and nerves. The middle apertures lie approximately 4 mm posterior to the equator and carry the vortex veins. The anterior apertures are near the limbus at the muscle insertions and are the passages for the anterior ciliary vessels, which are branches from the muscular arteries.

7. Scleral Blood Supply

Because it is relatively inactive metabolically, the sclera has a minimal blood supply. A number of vessels pass through the sclera to other tissues, but the sclera is considered avascular because it contains no capillary beds. Nourishment is furnished by small branches from the episcleral and choroidal vessels, and branches of the long posterior ciliary arteries.

8. Scleral Innervation

Sensory innervation is supplied to the posterior sclera by branches of the short ciliary nerves; the remainder of the sclera is served by branches of the long ciliary nerves.
